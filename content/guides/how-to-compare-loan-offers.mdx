## Why Comparing Matters

The same borrower — same credit score, same income, same debt — can receive loan offers ranging from 8% to 36% APR depending on which lender they apply with. On a $5,000 loan over 24 months, that difference looks like this:

- **At 8% APR:** $226/month, $5,416 total, **$416 in interest**
- **At 36% APR:** $253/month, $6,072 total, **$1,072 in interest**

<StatHighlight value="$656" label="difference in total cost on the same $5,000 loan — just by choosing a different lender" icon="dollar" variant="red" />

That is a $656 difference — simply because of which lender you chose. The monthly payment difference is only $27, which most people would shrug off. But the total cost difference is substantial.

Research from the [Consumer Financial Protection Bureau](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/ask-cfpb/) consistently shows that most borrowers accept the first offer they receive without shopping around. Do not be most people. Spending 30 to 60 minutes comparing offers is one of the highest-return uses of your time when borrowing money.

This guide walks you through a seven-step process for comparing loan offers so you can identify the cheapest option with the best terms — not just the one with the lowest monthly payment.

## Step 1: Get Multiple Pre-Qualified Offers

Before you can compare, you need offers to compare. Aim for **3 to 5 pre-qualified offers** from different types of lenders.

### Where to Get Offers

- **FundingZest:** [Submit one application](/apply) to see pre-qualified offers from multiple lenders in our network. One form, multiple results.
- **Credit unions:** If you are a member (or can join one), check their personal loan rates. Credit unions are nonprofit and often offer the lowest APRs available.
- **Online lenders:** Companies like LendingClub, Upstart, Prosper, and Avant allow pre-qualification with a soft credit pull.
- **Your bank:** If you have an existing relationship, ask about personal loan options. Some banks offer rate discounts for existing customers.

### Why Pre-Qualification Matters

Pre-qualification uses a **soft credit inquiry** that does not affect your credit score. This lets you see estimated rates and terms without any risk. A hard inquiry only happens when you formally accept an offer and proceed with a full application.

FICO scoring models also account for rate shopping. If you apply for multiple loans within a **14 to 45 day window** (depending on the scoring model), all of those inquiries are treated as a single inquiry. This means comparing aggressively within a short timeframe has minimal credit impact.

## Step 2: Compare APR (Not Interest Rate)

Once you have multiple offers, the first number to compare is the **APR** — not the interest rate.

The interest rate is the percentage the lender charges on your outstanding balance. The APR includes the interest rate **plus** certain mandatory fees (like origination fees), expressed as a single annualized figure. Two loans with the same interest rate can have very different APRs if one charges higher fees.

### Example: Why APR Reveals the True Cost

| | Loan A | Loan B |
|---|--------|--------|
| Loan amount | $5,000 | $5,000 |
| Interest rate | 12% | 14% |
| Origination fee | 3% ($150) | 0% ($0) |
| Effective APR | 15.8% | 14.0% |
| Term | 24 months | 24 months |
| Monthly payment | $240 | $239 |
| Total repaid | $5,760 | $5,736 |
| **Total cost of borrowing** | **$760** | **$736** |

Loan A has the lower interest rate (12% vs. 14%) but the higher APR and higher total cost because of the origination fee. If you compared interest rates alone, you would choose the more expensive loan.

**Always compare APR.** It is the standardized metric required by the federal Truth in Lending Act for exactly this reason. ## Step 3: Calculate Total Cost

APR tells you the annualized rate, but the **total cost** tells you the actual dollars leaving your pocket. This is the number that matters most for your budget.

**The formula is simple:**

Monthly payment x number of months = total amount paid

Total amount paid - amount borrowed = total cost of borrowing

Use our [loan calculator](/calculator) to run the numbers, or calculate manually. Here are three sample offers for a $3,000 loan to illustrate how APR and term length affect total cost:

| | Offer A | Offer B | Offer C |
|---|---------|---------|---------|
| Loan amount | $3,000 | $3,000 | $3,000 |
| APR | 15% | 20% | 12% |
| Term | 12 months | 12 months | 24 months |
| Monthly payment | $271 | $278 | $141 |
| Total repaid | $3,252 | $3,336 | $3,386 |
| **Total cost of borrowing** | **$252** | **$336** | **$386** |

Offer C has the lowest APR (12%) and the lowest monthly payment ($141) — but it costs the most overall ($386) because the term is twice as long. Offer A, with a higher APR than Offer C, is actually the cheapest loan because the shorter term means you pay interest for less time.

**The lesson:** Low monthly payments and low APR do not automatically mean low cost. Always calculate the total.

## Step 4: Review All Fees

Beyond APR and total cost, individual fees can significantly affect what you pay. Some fees are included in the APR calculation; others are not.

| Fee Type | Typical Range | Included in APR? | What to Watch For |
|----------|--------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Origination fee | 1% to 8% | Yes | May be deducted from your disbursement, reducing the cash you receive |
| Late payment fee | $15 to $39 or 5% of payment | No | Ask about grace period length before the fee applies |
| Prepayment penalty | Varies | No | Many online lenders charge none. Always confirm before signing |
| Returned payment (NSF) fee | $15 to $30 | No | Charged if your payment bounces due to insufficient funds |
| Check processing fee | $5 to $15 | No | Some lenders charge extra for non-ACH payment methods |
| Loan modification fee | Varies | No | Fee for changing your payment schedule or terms mid-loan |

<Callout type="tip" title="Key Question for Every Lender">
Ask: "Can you provide a **complete list of all fees** associated with this loan, including those that are conditional?" Any lender that cannot or will not answer this question clearly is a lender you should avoid.
</Callout>

## Step 5: Consider the Term Length

The loan term — how many months you have to repay — is one of the most important variables in your total cost. Shorter terms mean higher monthly payments but less total interest. Longer terms mean lower monthly payments but more total interest.

Here is a side-by-side comparison for a $5,000 loan at 15% APR across three different terms:

| | 12 Months | 24 Months | 36 Months |
|---|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Monthly payment | $451 | $242 | $173 |
| Total repaid | $5,416 | $5,818 | $6,238 |
| Total interest paid | $416 | $818 | $1,238 |
| Interest as % of principal | 8.3% | 16.4% | 24.8% |

The 36-month option has the most comfortable monthly payment ($173 vs. $451), but you pay nearly three times as much in total interest ($1,238 vs. $416). That extra $822 in interest is the price of lower monthly payments.

### How to Choose the Right Term

- **Choose the shortest term you can comfortably afford.** "Comfortably" means the payment fits your budget with room for unexpected expenses — not the absolute maximum you could pay if everything went perfectly.
- **Avoid stretching the term just to lower the payment.** If the only way to afford the loan is a 36-month term, you may be borrowing more than you should.
- **Consider your cash flow.** If your income varies month to month (freelance, gig work, seasonal), a slightly longer term with lower payments gives you more breathing room during lean months.

## Step 6: Check Lender Reputation

A low APR means nothing if the lender engages in deceptive practices, misapplies payments, or makes collections harassment a standard procedure. Before accepting any offer, verify the lender's reputation.

### What to Check

- **Better Business Bureau (BBB):** Look for the BBB rating and read recent complaints. Pay attention to how the company responds to complaints — resolution matters more than the complaint itself.
- **Online reviews:** Check Trustpilot, Google Reviews, and the Consumer Affairs website. Look for patterns, not individual reviews. Every lender has some negative reviews; consistent themes (billing errors, hidden fees, poor customer service) are the red flags.
- **State licensing:** Verify the lender is licensed to operate in your state through your state's Department of Financial Institutions or Division of Banking. Unlicensed lenders are not subject to state consumer protection laws.
- **CFPB complaint database:** The [Consumer Financial Protection Bureau](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/ask-cfpb/) maintains a public database of consumer complaints against financial companies. Search for the lender and review both the volume and nature of complaints.

### Red Flags

- Multiple unresolved complaints about hidden fees or unexpected charges
- Reviews describing aggressive collection practices for accounts that are not severely delinquent
- No physical address or customer service phone number on the website
- The lender contacts you first through unsolicited calls, emails, or texts
- Guaranteed approval regardless of credit — legitimate lenders always evaluate creditworthiness
- Pressure to sign immediately or claims that the offer expires within hours

The [FTC's consumer advice on borrowing and credit](https://consumer.ftc.gov/credit-loans-and-debt) provides additional resources for identifying and reporting predatory lenders.

## Step 7: Read the Fine Print

Before you accept any offer, read the loan agreement in full. This is the legally binding document that governs your loan. Everything the lender told you verbally or displayed on their website is secondary to what the agreement says.

### What to Look For

- **Rate type:** Confirm whether the rate is fixed or variable. If variable, what index is it tied to and is there a cap?
- **Payment schedule:** Verify the payment amount, due date, and frequency match what you were quoted.
- **Default terms:** What constitutes a default? Some agreements define default as one missed payment; others allow a grace period.
- **Late fee structure:** Confirm the fee amount, when it applies, and whether there is a grace period.
- **Prepayment terms:** Verify in writing that there is no prepayment penalty (or understand the penalty if there is one).
- **Arbitration clause:** Many loan agreements include mandatory arbitration clauses that prevent you from suing the lender in court. Understand whether this applies and what it means for your rights.
- **Automatic payment authorization:** Some agreements authorize the lender to withdraw payments from your bank account. Understand the terms of this authorization and how to revoke it if needed.

If anything in the agreement contradicts what the lender told you during the application process, do not sign until the discrepancy is resolved in writing.

## Common Comparison Mistakes

Even informed borrowers make these mistakes when evaluating loan offers. Avoid them.

<Callout type="warning" title="Don't Fall for the Monthly Payment Trap">
The lowest monthly payment often comes with the longest term, meaning you pay **more** in total interest. Always calculate the total cost of the loan, not just what fits your monthly budget.
</Callout>

### Mistake 1: Focusing Only on Monthly Payment

The monthly payment is part of the picture, not the whole picture. As shown in Step 3, a $141/month payment over 24 months costs more than a $271/month payment over 12 months for the same loan amount. Always look at total cost.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring Fees

Origination fees, late fees, and prepayment penalties are real costs that affect what you pay. A loan advertised as "low interest" with a 6% origination fee may be more expensive than a higher-rate loan with no fees.

### Mistake 3: Not Comparing Enough Offers

Accepting the first offer you receive is the single most common — and most costly — borrowing mistake. Every additional offer you collect gives you more data and more leverage. Three offers is the minimum; five is better.

### Mistake 4: Not Reading the Loan Agreement

The marketing materials and the loan agreement are not the same document. Rates, fees, and terms in the agreement are what you are legally bound to. Read it before signing.

### Mistake 5: Choosing Based on Brand Name Alone

A well-known bank is not automatically the best lender for your situation. Credit unions and online lenders frequently offer lower rates and fewer fees than national banks. Compare on numbers, not name recognition.
